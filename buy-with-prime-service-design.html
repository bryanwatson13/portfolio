01 — Service Challenge

A delivery system built for one type of customer

Amazon's Buy with Prime promises seamless shopping across third-party merchants. But the promise breaks the moment a customer doesn't live in a single-family home. Apartment dwellers, students, and urban residents face a structural gap: they cannot reliably receive packages at their front door.

This wasn't just a UX problem. It was a service gap that touched multiple actors, backstage systems, physical infrastructure, and partner relationships — all of which needed to be understood holistically before any screen was designed.

From product feature to service question

The original brief asked for a locker pick-up feature. A service design lens reframes this: How might we design a pick-up service that reliably serves customers across diverse living situations, through every touchpoint — digital and physical?

The screen is only the last 10% of the experience. The other 90% lives in logistics, partner agreements, locker hardware, and customer trust.

02 — Service Ecosystem

Mapping the actors & relationships

Before defining any solution, the team mapped every actor involved in delivering a successful locker pick-up experience — from the merchant on Kitzy.com to the Bluetooth hardware inside the Amazon Hub.

End User

Buy with Prime Customer

Urban/apartment resident who cannot receive home delivery reliably. Has Prime account, shops third-party merchants.

Platform

Buy with Prime

Amazon's service enabling Prime benefits (shipping, payments) on external merchant sites.

Third-Party Merchant

e.g. Kitzy.com

Independent retailer using Buy with Prime to offer Prime checkout. Has no control over fulfillment routing.

Physical Partner

Amazon Hub Locker

Physical locker infrastructure in retail locations. Bluetooth-enabled, app-connected. Hosts "Katie," "Evan," and other named units.

The Service

Locker Pick-Up via Buy with Prime

Fulfillment

Amazon Logistics / Flex

Delivery drivers using the Flex app to deposit packages into correct lockers. Triggers customer notification.

Host Location

Retail / Amazon Go

Physical spaces housing lockers. Holiday hours, access restrictions, and lobby placement affect customer experience.

Support

Amazon Customer Service

Handles failed pick-ups, incorrect items, and locker access failures. A critical recovery touchpoint.

Technology

Google Maps API

Powers locker search and "View location in maps" — a third-party dependency in the pick-up flow.

03 — Research & Discovery

Listening before designing

Research drew on competitive auditing (Amazon's own non-Buy with Prime locker flow, Target, Walmart) and social listening. The goal was to understand not just what users wanted, but why they wanted it — which revealed three distinct motivational segments.

Competitors audited included Amazon's native locker flow, Target Drive Up, and Walmart Pickup — all services with more mature pick-up infrastructure to learn from.

Three motivations. Three service needs.

01

Convenience-Driven

"I pick it up while running errands." These users treat the locker as a flexible delivery hub woven into daily routines — not a workaround.

02

Security-Driven

"Package theft is a real problem where I live." These users choose lockers as a safer alternative to porch delivery — trust and reliability are paramount.

03

Privacy-Driven

"I prefer the anonymity." These users actively choose not to have packages tied to a home address — discretion is the service value, not just convenience.

06 — Opportunity Areas

Where the service can be strengthened

The service blueprint reveals gaps that screen design alone cannot solve. These opportunity areas span digital, operational, and partnership layers.

Digital Touchpoint

Reduce locker selection friction

Users exit to Google Maps mid-flow to verify location details — a trust gap. Embedding richer location data (photos, lobby instructions, real-time availability) directly in the pick-up UI keeps users in-flow and builds confidence before committing.

Backstage Operation

Real-time locker availability sync

If a locker is shown as available but is actually full, the entire service fails at the physical touchpoint. A live inventory sync between locker hardware and the selection UI would prevent misdirected orders and eliminate a critical failure point.

Partner & Physical Layer

Host location communication standards

Locker access hours differ from host location hours (Amazon Go, retail partners). Holiday closures create abandoned pick-ups. Standardizing how hours are surfaced — and proactively notifying customers of restrictions — would reduce physical-layer failures.

Notification & Recovery

Proactive failure recovery journeys

When a pick-up fails (wrong item, offline locker, expired window), there is no clear recovery path in the service. A dedicated recovery flow — in-app and via notification — would turn a service failure into a trust-building moment.

07 — Design Decisions Through a Service Lens

Why each design choice served the broader service

The UX decisions in this project weren't just about screen usability — each was a response to a service-level challenge revealed through the blueprint and journey mapping.

Decision 01

Address selection as the ingress point

Rather than a separate "locker mode," introducing pick-up through the existing address selection screen kept the service model familiar. Users understand "where do you want it sent?" — this reframes the locker as just another valid address, reducing cognitive overhead.

Service implication: fewer customers drop off due to an unfamiliar checkout paradigm.

Decision 02

Locker detail sheets with instructions

Physical instructions ("find the locker in the entry vestibule") were surfaced before the user committed to a location — bridging the digital-to-physical gap. This pre-arrival context reduces locker-floor confusion and failed pick-ups.

Service implication: reduces calls to customer support for "I can't find the locker."

Decision 03

Accessibility filtering (wheelchair icon)

The accessibility filter on locker search reflects a service commitment to inclusivity. Locker placement within host locations varies — embedding accessibility data at selection time ensures the physical environment matches the digital promise.

Service implication: the service works for customers who need physical accessibility accommodations.

Design iterations as service hypotheses

Multiple design directions were tested — card-based vs. link-based locker lists, map-first vs. list-first layouts, expanded detail sheets vs. inline snippets. Each test was not just a usability test but a service experiment: which design reduces the likelihood of a failed pick-up?

The recommended ingress (through address selection, not a separate flow) emerged as the design that best served the three user motivations — convenience, security, and privacy — while aligning with backstage fulfillment systems that already route by address.

09 — Service Design Reflections

What this project taught me about designing services

Constraints exist in the backstage, not just the UI

The biggest design decisions weren't about screens — they were about what backstage systems could support. Engineering limitations shaped the ingress point. A service designer must understand and design within the full operational system.

Physical evidence is a design material

The locker's physical location within a store, its signage, its hours — these are service design materials just as much as any UI component. The locker detail sheet in the app was a bridge between the digital interface and the physical environment.

Different motivations need different service emphases

Convenience, security, and privacy users share the same flow but need different assurances at different points. A service design approach surfaces these needs early, so the UI and operational layers can be calibrated accordingly.

Recovery journeys are part of the service design

A locker pick-up will sometimes fail. The absence of a clear recovery path is itself a design decision — a bad one. Future iterations should map and design failure recovery as a first-class part of the service.
