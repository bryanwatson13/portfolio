01 — Service Challenge

A beloved local store with a broken digital service

Gauntlet Comic Books is a North Miami retailer that has built a loyal in-store following among comic enthusiasts. But the store's digital presence — its website — was actively driving customers away. Sales and subscription goals were not being met, limiting revenue and growth potential.

The original brief asked for a website redesign. A service design lens asks a bigger question: why is the digital channel failing, and how does that failure ripple through the entire retail service — from publisher relationships to subscription fulfillment to customer loyalty?

From web redesign to service question

Comic book retail is not just a transactional service. It involves recurring subscriptions (pull lists), pre-orders months in advance, physical inventory management, community building, and a passionate customer base with highly specific needs. The website wasn't just poorly designed — it was disconnected from the service the store actually provided.

Comic book sites have terrible customer service. The site doesn't tell me what's in stock, what's new, or how to subscribe.

02 — Service Ecosystem

Mapping all actors in the comic retail service

Before designing a single screen, the team mapped every actor involved in delivering Gauntlet's retail service — from publishers and distributors to the end customer and the subscription system.

End Customer

Comic Book Reader

Average age 35–44, nearly half female — a demographic broader than stereotypes suggest. Shops online (2 in 3 buy from online stores), prefers physical print, and values pre-order availability.

Retailer

Gauntlet Comic Books

Local North Miami retailer offering single issues, back-runs, subscriptions (pull lists), and merchandise. Has physical store and website; revenue depends on both channels.

Subscription Service

Pull List / Pre-Order

A recurring subscription model where customers reserve upcoming issues. Popular (16.7% of fans pre-order) but broken on the website — subscribing was one of the three failed user tasks.

Upstream

Publishers & Distributors

Marvel, DC, Image, and others release new issues weekly. Inventory availability and new release data must flow from distributor to retailer to website in near real-time.

The Service

Gauntlet's Online Retail & Subscription Experience

Competition

Amazon & Midtown Comics

Amazon captures 43% of online comic purchases; Midtown Comics takes 11%. These are the default destinations when Gauntlet's service fails to deliver. Best-in-class (Comics Etc, Barnes & Noble) set the benchmark.

Community

Hobbyist & Fan Networks

Social media groups, local Slack channels, and fan communities. These are research channels and word-of-mouth drivers — currently underutilized by Gauntlet's digital service.

Physical Channel

The Physical Store

The origin of the business and its primary trust signal. But 60.7% of fans don't use reviews when choosing a comic — personal discovery and browsability matters more.

Digital Channel

The Website

The broken service touchpoint. No clear path, ineffective search, out-of-stock products surfacing in results, inconsistent CTAs, and no orientation for new visitors.

03 — Research & Discovery

The most research-intensive phase of the project

The team conducted a sweeping research effort across six methods: a UX audit of the existing site, a competitive audit (in and out of category), social media listening, a 60+ response survey, industry data analysis, and 1:1 user interviews with task completion testing.

The research revealed that the comic book customer is far more diverse than expected — and that the service gap wasn't a lack of demand, but a broken delivery mechanism.

Three research-driven user segments

01

The Series Collector

Follows specific characters, writers, and publishers. Needs back-run availability, complete storyline bundles, and cross-over event information. Pre-ordering is critical to not miss issues.

02

The Casual Browser

Arrives at the site without a specific title in mind. Needs discoverability — new releases, trending titles, and curated entry points by genre, character, or publisher. Easily lost on the current site.

03

The Merchandise & Gift Buyer

Not primarily a reader — shops for related merchandise or gifts. Needs clear product categories, suggestions based on popular characters, and a frictionless checkout. Currently invisible on the site.

What the UX audit revealed about service failure

The UX audit of Gauntlet's existing site mapped specific failure points across three pages. These weren't just design problems — each represented a point where the service broke down entirely:

Homepage: Products were buried beneath a large hero image of the store interior. The cart was unfindable. New releases — the most time-sensitive service value a comic retailer has — were absent from the main page entirely.

Search Results: Items listed as out of stock appeared in results, wasting customer time and eroding trust. The faceted search was misaligned with the page layout, and navigation links failed to show the user's current location.

Product Detail Page: Minimal product information, no peer reviews, no related merchandise suggestions, and misleading CTAs in a different color than the rest of the site. The page lacked personality — a critical failure for a product category driven by passion and fandom.

Task testing: Users were asked to find a book, add it to the cart, and subscribe to a pull list. All three tasks were difficult or failed, with many abandoning the site mid-task to search elsewhere.

06 — Service Opportunity Areas

Where the service needs to change

The MVP prioritization session (value vs. effort chart) helped the team sequence opportunities. Service design reframes these as gaps across the full service system — not just features to add to a website.

Inventory & Discovery

Real-time stock as a service signal

Showing out-of-stock items in search results is not just a UI bug — it's a service failure that breaks trust and causes defection. Live inventory sync between the distributor feed and the website search index is the foundational fix before any design work matters.

Subscription Service

Pull list as a loyalty service, not a form

16.7% of fans pre-order titles. The pull list is Gauntlet's strongest retention tool — recurring, habitual, and relationship-building. Redesigning it as a proactive subscription service (with notifications, new issue alerts, and storyline tracking) transforms it from a form to a service.

Search & Navigation

Multiple entry points for diverse readers

The 47% female, 35–44 demographic shops differently than the stereotypical young male collector. Multiple search categories (publishers, characters, genre, writers, artists), autocomplete, and curated entry points serve the full breadth of the customer base — not just the core fan.

Checkout & Conversion

Streamlined checkout as competitive parity

Amazon's 1-click advantage is a service benchmark. A progress bar, Apple Pay/Google Pay support, and the ability to buy directly from the product page — bypassing the cart entirely for single purchases — close the conversion gap with competing retailers.

07 — Design Decisions Through a Service Lens

Why each design choice served the broader service

Decision 01

Featured new releases on homepage

New issue Wednesday is the heartbeat of comic retail. Featuring new releases on the homepage aligns the digital service with the physical store's most important weekly event — giving the website a reason to be visited regularly, not just for one-time transactions.

Service implication: increases return visits and subscription discoverability.

Decision 02

Subscribe button on every current title

Placing a subscribe-to-pull-list button on every active title card removes the service barrier between discovery and commitment. The customer doesn't need to navigate to a separate subscription page — the service wraps around their browsing behavior.

Service implication: pull list conversion happens at the moment of interest, not at a later, lower-intent step.

Decision 03

Engage · Facilitate · Convert UX strategy

The three-pillar strategy — make it Fun, make it Easy, make it Read — is a service design principle, not just a visual direction. Engage means the service reflects the passion of the product. Facilitate means the service removes barriers. Convert means the service earns trust at checkout.

Service implication: every design decision maps back to a service outcome, not just an aesthetic preference.

The MVP session as service triage

The value vs. effort chart — produced in a collaborative workshop — was a service prioritization exercise, not just a feature backlog. High-value, low-effort items like autocomplete search, a subscribe button on every title, and streamlined checkout addressed the three service failure points directly. High-value, high-effort items like animated hero transitions and a BuzzFeed-style quiz were deferred — important for the Engage pillar, but not the foundational service fixes needed first.

09 — Service Design Reflections

What this project revealed about local retail service design

Assumptions about who uses a service can undermine it

47% of comic book readers are female, and the average age is 35–44. Designing a service around the assumed customer — young, male, deeply nerdy — systematically fails the majority. Research that challenges assumptions is a prerequisite for service design that works.

Small retailers need service design more than anyone

Unlike Amazon or Midtown Comics, Gauntlet has no algorithmic recommendation engine or logistics network. Its competitive advantage is community, expertise, and locality — but those values only translate online if the service is intentionally designed to carry them.

The pull list is a service design problem, not a feature problem

Adding a subscribe button was the design solution. The service design insight is bigger: subscriptions require notification systems, inventory predictability, publisher coordination, and customer trust — none of which the existing digital service supported.

Connecting with niche communities accelerates research

The perceived obstacle — "comic books are a small market, we won't find enough data" — was overcome by reaching into hobbyist Slack channels and fan groups. Service designers who go where users actually gather get richer, faster, and more honest data than those who wait for it to come to them.
