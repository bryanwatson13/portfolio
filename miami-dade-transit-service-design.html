01 — Service Challenge

A public service that its own riders couldn't access

The Miami-Dade County Transit Store is a government-operated digital service that allows riders to purchase Easy Cards, reload transit passes, register Parking Permits, and manage their transit accounts. Despite serving a daily transit ridership of hundreds of thousands, the site was barely being used.

Consumers were not utilizing the website to learn about or purchase these products — and transit ridership in Miami had declined 25% since 2019, with bus route cuts, ghost buses, and schedule unreliability already eroding trust in the system itself.

From website redesign to public service question

The Transit Store isn't a retail site competing for discretionary spending. It's a critical access point for a public infrastructure service. When it fails, riders can't reload their cards conveniently, can't buy permits online, and must go to physical kiosks — even if they don't ride the train. The service design question is: how do we design a digital channel that earns the trust of riders who already distrust the transit system, and makes managing transit genuinely easier than going to the kiosk?

People aren't buying Easy Passes online because they don't want to deal with this. The website doesn't work — and neither, sometimes, does the transit.

02 — Service Ecosystem

A complex public service with many actors

The Transit Store sits at the intersection of a government agency, physical infrastructure, multiple fare products, and a diverse ridership. Mapping these relationships reveals why the digital channel can't be fixed in isolation.

Primary User

Transit Rider

Daily bus and rail commuters, park-and-ride users. Diverse demographics — many with long commutes, limited time, and existing frustration with system reliability. 75% use the Easy Card system; 85% reload at the station.

Government Agency

Miami-Dade County Transit (MDT)

Operator of the bus, Metrorail, and Metromover systems. Stakeholder in ridership growth, fare collection, and site performance (tracked via Google Analytics).

Fare Product

Easy Card

A reloadable transit card used for bus, rail, and Metromover. Also unlocks discounts at local restaurants and events. Can be purchased and reloaded online or at station kiosks.

Fare Product

Parking Permit

Digital permit allowing riders to park at park-and-ride lots near train stations. $40/day without a permit at Brickell. High-value product with a confusing online purchase path.

The Transit Store

Digital service channel for Easy Cards, Parking Permits & merchandise

Competing Channel

Station Kiosk

The default card reload point for 85% of riders. Perceived as more reliable than the website. The Transit Store must out-convenient the kiosk to win adoption.

Secondary Need

Merchandise

60% of surveyed riders expressed interest in purchasing MDT-branded merchandise — an untapped revenue stream not previously offered through the digital channel.

Best-in-Class Reference

NY/Boston/Tokyo Transit + Disney/SunPass

Benchmarked for clear CTAs, iconographic product representation, guided checkouts, and reloadable card UX. Over 30 sites analyzed.

Support

Customer Service & Language Access

Miami's multilingual population requires the service to work in multiple languages — a positive from the existing site that must be preserved in the redesign.

Three rider personas with distinct service needs

01

The Daily Commuter

25-year-old part-time worker, married, bus-dependent for work and school. Needs to reload quickly without going to the station. Can't check card balance without logging in. Values speed and convenience above all else.

02

The Park-and-Ride Driver

Matt, 36, accountant in downtown Miami. Pays $40/day to park near work — transit is a financial no-brainer but the parking permit process is opaque and frustrating online. Wants a streamlined digital path to manage his permit.

03

The Occasional Visitor

New or infrequent transit user who needs orientation — what is an Easy Card, how does it work, what are the benefits? The existing site provides no onboarding. A first-time visitor has no idea what to do or why they should.

What the UX audit revealed about the existing service

Positives to preserve: The existing site had genuine strengths — multiple language support (critical for Miami's diverse population), good copywriting, and engaging imagery. These were anchors for the redesign, not things to throw away.

Navigation failure: Redundant navigation links with no clear hierarchy. Users reported not knowing where they were on the site at any point, with links that didn't indicate active state or logical relationship.

No clear path: The site offered no guided route from arrival to purchase. A rider who wanted to buy a Parking Permit encountered a broken link that sent them to the License Account page — requiring separate account creation before any transaction could happen.

No CTAs and no orientation: The homepage had no clear calls to action distinguishing Easy Card from Parking Permit from Reload. First-time visitors had no indication of what to do or where to start — the service provided no welcome.

06 — Service Opportunity Areas

Where the public transit service can be rebuilt

The UX strategy — Clear, Convincing, Modern — maps directly to service design principles. Each pillar addresses a specific layer of the service failure uncovered in research.

Discovery & Awareness

A service riders can find before they need it

40% awareness is a service design failure, not a marketing failure. Integrating Transit Store awareness into the Easy Card registration process, station kiosk screens, and MDT app onboarding turns the digital channel from a hidden option into a default one. Future iterations should include onboarding screens for first-time visitors who arrive directly.

Homepage & Navigation

Clear product iconography as service wayfinding

Easy Cards, Parking Permits, and Merchandise are three distinct products with distinct purchase paths. Iconographic distinction on the homepage — borrowed from best-in-class transit systems like Tokyo and Boston — lets riders self-select their path immediately, without reading copy. Navigation should shrink to the essential three, not expand.

Checkout & Trust

Competing with the kiosk on convenience

The station kiosk reload is the benchmark the digital service must beat. A streamlined checkout (progress bar, fewer steps, pre-filled account details for returning riders) combined with Apple Pay and Google Pay support makes online reload faster and more convenient than going to the station — the only argument that will shift rider behavior.

Account & Retention

Proactive card management as a service benefit

Low-balance notifications, auto-reload options, and balance checking without login transform the Transit Store from a one-time transaction site into an ongoing service relationship. The Easy Card's discount benefit (local restaurants, shopping, events) is an underutilized loyalty hook that the site should actively surface.

07 — Design Decisions Through a Service Lens

Clear · Convincing · Modern — as service design principles

Clear

Product iconography on the homepage

Three distinct product categories — Easy Cards, Parking Permits, Shop — represented by icons on the homepage acts as service wayfinding. Riders don't have to read or interpret; they see and click. This decision was informed by best-in-class transit sites and by interview data showing riders arrived confused about what the site even offered.

Convincing

Benefits accessible from the homepage

Survey data showed riders appreciate the Easy Card discount benefits (restaurants, events, shopping) but don't actively know about them. Surfacing benefits from the homepage is a service design decision: it answers the implicit rider question "why should I use this site?" before they have to ask it. Trust is built before a transaction is requested.

Modern

A visual style users called "very Miami"

The style guide — hot pink CTAs, dark navy backgrounds, clean iconography — was tested with over 15 users and described as feeling "very Miami." This isn't vanity design. A government site that feels modern and alive signals to riders that the service is maintained, active, and trustworthy — directly countering the trust deficit created by years of broken links and 25% ridership decline.

The current vs. proposed user flow as a service design argument

The most concrete service design artifact in this project was the comparison between the current and proposed user flows for purchasing a Parking Permit. The current flow required: Homepage → Account? → Click Login → Login Page → Sign In → Dashboard → Link Broken → Link License → License Account Page → Add New → License Page → Manage License. Twelve steps, a broken link, and account creation before any product could be purchased.

The proposed flow: Homepage → Click Buy → Parking Permit PDP → Click Buy → Checkout (name, license number, easy pass number, payment info, create account) → Confirmation. Five steps, product-first, account creation deferred to checkout. The new sitemap reduced navigation options and made purchasing available directly from sub-menus — a structural service improvement, not a visual one.

09 — Service Design Reflections

What public sector service design demands

Government services carry a trust deficit that design must address

Riders didn't avoid the Transit Store because the website was ugly — they avoided it because Miami-Dade Transit had lost their trust through route cuts, ghost buses, and schedule failures. The visual strategy ("very Miami," modern iconography) was a service trust signal, not decoration. Design that feels current tells riders the agency is paying attention.

Competing with kiosk behavior requires beating it on convenience

85% of riders reload at the station kiosk, even when they don't take the train. The kiosk is their mental default. The only way to change that behavior is to make the digital service provably faster and more convenient — not just available. The streamlined checkout, auto-reload, and balance notifications are the service design levers that make that case.

Unmet demand can be the most surprising research finding

The biggest fear going into this project was that riders didn't want to use the site — that the reputation problem was too deep. The survey revealed the opposite: riders want to use it, but the site doesn't work. That reframe — from "convince reluctant users" to "remove barriers for willing ones" — changed the entire design strategy.

Competing CTAs on a homepage are a service architecture problem

The concern that Easy Cards, Parking Permits, and Merchandise would create competing CTAs on the homepage turned out to be solvable through iconographic hierarchy rather than simplification. Service design teaches that multiple distinct products can coexist if each has a clear visual pathway — the three-column homepage structure answered this without removing any product.
